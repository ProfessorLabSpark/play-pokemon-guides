# Tournament Structure

This chapter explains how Play! Pokémon tournaments are structured from a judge’s operational perspective. It focuses on how TOM groups players, determines rounds, and transitions into Top Cut for Championship Series events. This is not a replacement for the Tournament Rules Handbook; it clarifies how judges interact with TOM and what to expect during event operations.

---

## Age Divisions

Play! Pokémon events always use three age divisions:

- Juniors  
- Seniors  
- Masters  

These divisions always remain separate, even when players are paired together in a unified pod. Divisions remain separate for:

- Standings  
- Final placement  
- Championship Points (for Championship Series events)

Pairings may cross divisions, but standings and CP never do.

---

## Legal Cross‑Division Pairings

TOM only allows the following cross‑division pairings:

- Juniors ↔ Seniors  
- Seniors ↔ Masters  

TOM will never pair:

- Masters ↔ Juniors  

Seniors act as the “bridge” division.

---

## Pod Formation Logic

Judges do not configure pods, but they should understand how divisions get grouped once the Organizer selects the number of pods.

Each division forms its own pod if it has 6 or more players.  
Divisions with fewer than 6 players combine with the next‑closest age division they are allowed to pair with.  
If no legal combination reaches 6, all divisions collapse into one unified pod.

### 1. Juniors
1. If Juniors ≥ 6 → Junior pod  
2. Else if Juniors + Seniors ≥ 6 → Junior/Senior pod  
3. Else → All divisions combined into one unified pod  
   (because Juniors cannot combine with Masters)

### 2. Seniors  
(Only evaluated if not already unified)

1. If Seniors ≥ 6 → Senior pod  
2. Else if Seniors + Juniors ≥ 6 → Senior/Junior pod  
3. Else if Seniors + Masters ≥ 6 → Senior/Master pod  
4. Else → All remaining divisions combined into one unified pod

### 3. Masters  
(Only evaluated if not already unified)

1. If Masters ≥ 6 → Master pod  
2. Else if Masters + Seniors ≥ 6 → Master/Senior pod  
3. Else → All remaining divisions combined into one unified pod  
   (Masters cannot combine with Juniors)

---

## Swiss Rounds

Swiss rounds determine standings without eliminating players early. All players in a pod play the same number of rounds unless they drop from the event.

Swiss rounds are calculated per pod, not per event.

---

## How TOM Determines the Number of Swiss Rounds

TOM uses a two‑stage process.

### Stage 1 — Initial Round Count (Before Round 1 Pairings Are Generated)

At the start of the tournament, TOM sets the number of rounds for each pod based on:

1. The largest age division (with 2 or more players)  
2. Inside that pod  
3. Using the Swiss Round Table

Example:  
If the largest division in a pod has 12 players, TOM sets 4 rounds.

---

### Stage 2 — Re‑Evaluation Before Each Round’s Pairings Are Generated

Before generating pairings for the next round, TOM checks:

**Does the number of active players in this pod still meet the threshold required for the originally scheduled number of rounds?**

If the active player count has dropped below the threshold for the next Swiss bracket size, TOM reduces the total number of rounds.

Judges should not promise a fixed number of rounds until TOM generates the next round.

---

## Swiss Round Table

| Players | Rounds |
|---------|--------|
| 1       | 0 |
| 2–3     | 2 |
| 4–8     | 3 |
| 9–16    | 4 |
| 17–32   | 5 |
| 33–64   | 6 |
| 65–128  | 7 |

This table is applied to the largest division with 2 or more players in each pod.

---

## How Swiss Pairings Work

- Players with the same or similar match records are paired together.  
- TOM avoids repeat pairings whenever possible.  
- Byes are assigned only when necessary.  
- A bye always goes to the lowest‑ranked player who has not yet received one.  
- Cross‑division pairings follow age‑division pairing rules:
  - Juniors may be paired with Seniors  
  - Seniors may be paired with Masters  
  - Masters are never paired with Juniors

---

## Judge Responsibilities During Swiss

Judges do not manually verify pairing legality or Swiss pairing logic. TOM implements all pairing rules defined by Play! Pokémon.

Judges also do not process player drops. Players must report drops to the Organizer or Scorekeeper, who updates TOM.

Judges should:

- Ensure players are seated at the correct tables  
- Ensure match results are reported accurately  
- Maintain tournament flow and pacing  
- Handle judge calls and resolve game state issues  
- Support the Organizer if pods have different round counts (staffing permitting)  
- Be prepared to help answer player questions if TOM reduces the total number of rounds, even though communicating structural changes is primarily the Organizer’s responsibility  
- Escalate player concerns about pairings or byes to the Organizer or Head Judge (if present), rather than attempting to manually validate or recalculate pairings  
- Direct players to the Organizer/Scorekeeper for drops, registration issues, or TOM-related corrections  

---

## Examples

### Example A — Single Pod, 17 Players

Largest division with 2+ players = Masters (12)  
Initial round count = 5 rounds

Before Round 4 pairings, active players drop to 15.  
Threshold for 5 rounds is no longer met.  
TOM reduces the event to 4 rounds.

---

### Example B — Two Pods With Different Round Counts

Pod A: 8 players → 3 rounds  
Pod B: 12 players → 4 rounds

Judges support the Organizer in managing each pod as staffing allows.  
Pod A will finish earlier than Pod B.

---

### Example C — 1‑Player Division

1 Junior, 7 Seniors, 12 Masters  
Pod A: Juniors + Seniors → 3 rounds  
Pod B: Masters → 4 rounds

The Junior still plays in Pod A’s rounds but has their own standings and placement.

# Two‑Phase Tournaments (Championship Series Events)

League Cups and higher‑level events use a two‑phase structure:

- Phase 1: Swiss Rounds  
- Phase 2: Single‑Elimination Top Cut  

This section clarifies how the transition works and what judges need to know.

---

## Top Cut Qualification

Top Cut is determined **only** by the number of active players in each age division at the end of Swiss.

It is not based on:

- Pod size  
- Total event attendance  
- Number of players who started the event  

Each division qualifies its own Top Cut independently.

### Examples

- Masters started with 42 players but only 38 remain active → Top 8  
- Seniors started with 9 players but only 7 remain active → Top 4  
- Juniors started with 5 players and all 5 remain → No Top Cut (Swiss only)

TOM handles this automatically.

---

## Swiss → Top Cut Transition

At the end of the final Swiss round:

1. TOM finalizes standings for each division  
2. Players confirm standings  
3. The Organizer announces who made Top Cut  
4. Judges prepare for Phase 2 (deck checks, seating, etc.)

Judges do not determine who makes Top Cut.  
TOM uses match points and tiebreakers.

---

## Top Cut Structure

- Single‑elimination  
- Format (BO1 or BO3) is chosen by the Organizer  
- If BO3 is used, rounds are typically 75 minutes  
- No ties  
- Sudden death if needed  
- Seeding is fixed (1 vs 8, 2 vs 7, etc.)

Judges enforce procedures; TOM handles pairings.

---

## Judge Responsibilities in Phase 2

Judges should:

- Assist with deck checks as assigned  
- Ensure players sit at the correct tables  
- Enforce time limits and slow play policy  
- Handle appeals (Head Judge)  
- Ensure no Top Cut match ends in a tie  
- Apply sudden death correctly when required  
- Maintain a clear, distraction‑free environment  
- Support the Organizer in communicating structure and expectations  

Judges do not:

- Decide who makes Top Cut  
- Modify standings  
- Adjust seeding  
- Override TOM’s Top Cut bracket  

---

## Common Player Misunderstandings (Judges Should Be Ready For)

- “Why didn’t I make Top Cut? I had the same record.”  
  → Tiebreakers determine standings.

- “Why is Top Cut best‑of‑3 when Swiss was best‑of‑1?”  
  → This is an Organizer decision. Swiss and Top Cut formats are configurable and do not have to match.

- “Why is the round 75 minutes?”  
  → This is the standard time used when an Organizer selects Best‑of‑3.

- “Why can’t Top Cut matches end in a tie?”  
  → Single‑elimination requires a winner.

- “Why is Top Cut based on division size and not pod size?”  
  → Pods are pairing structures; divisions determine advancement.
